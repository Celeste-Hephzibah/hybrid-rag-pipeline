Machine learning is a field of artificial intelligence that enables systems to learn patterns from data rather than following explicit rules. Supervised learning trains a model on labeled examples, where each input is paired with a known correct output. Common supervised learning tasks include classification, such as spam detection, and regression, such as predicting house prices. Unsupervised learning, by contrast, works with unlabeled data and tries to discover hidden structure, such as clustering customers into segments based on purchasing behavior.

Deep learning is a subset of machine learning based on artificial neural networks with multiple layers. Convolutional neural networks, or CNNs, are especially effective for image data because they exploit spatial locality through shared filters. Recurrent neural networks and, more recently, transformer architectures are widely used for sequential data such as text and time series. The transformer architecture, introduced in the paper "Attention Is All You Need," relies on self-attention mechanisms rather than recurrence, which allows for much greater parallelization during training.

Retrieval-Augmented Generation, or RAG, combines a retrieval system with a generative language model. Instead of relying solely on knowledge baked into the model's parameters during training, a RAG system retrieves relevant documents or passages at query time and conditions the generated answer on that retrieved context. This reduces hallucination and allows the system to answer questions about information that postdates the model's training cutoff, or that is private to an organization and was never part of the training data at all.

Hybrid search in the context of RAG refers to combining dense vector retrieval, which captures semantic similarity, with sparse retrieval methods like BM25, which capture exact keyword and lexical overlap. Dense retrieval alone can miss exact matches on rare terms, acronyms, or product codes because embedding models tend to smooth over such details. BM25 alone can miss semantically related passages that use different wording than the query. Combining both, typically through a fusion method like Reciprocal Rank Fusion, tends to outperform either method used in isolation.

A cross-encoder reranker is often added as a second stage after hybrid retrieval. Unlike a bi-encoder, which embeds the query and each document independently and compares them with a similarity metric, a cross-encoder processes the query and document together in a single forward pass, allowing the model to directly attend between them. This produces more accurate relevance judgments at the cost of being too slow to run over an entire corpus, which is why it is typically only applied to a small shortlist of candidates that a faster retrieval stage has already narrowed down.
